worth it? methodology

Every score on this site is built from published fiber science and available product data. This page documents exactly how scores are estimated so you can scrutinize the methodology, identify where confidence is limited, and hold us accountable. v1.1

What this card is for

This is a shopping comparison aid, not a standalone quality calculator. It is designed to help you answer one practical question: is this item's material quality in line with its price, and would a similar product at this price likely hold up better?

The score does not declare an item "good" or "bad" in the abstract. It estimates how a given fiber composition typically wears, then weighs that against what the price implies — so you can judge a specific item against the next-best alternative you might buy instead. Every figure below is an estimate based on available product data, not a guarantee about any individual garment.

What scores measure — and what they don't

What fiber science can tell you (high confidence): how a specific fiber composition behaves over time — pilling rate, tensile strength, colorfastness, moisture management. These properties are measurable by ASTM standards and are consistent across garments made from the same fiber.

What fiber science cannot fully tell you: how well a specific garment was constructed. A $20 cotton t-shirt and a $120 cotton t-shirt share the same fiber score. Construction quality — seam type, stitch density, lining — is not part of the Material Durability score. We estimate it separately (see Construction Score below) from price tier and any construction signals in the product description or image, and fold that estimate into the final Worth-It Score. It remains an estimate: precise construction quality requires physical inspection.

What price pressure tells you: whether the price you're paying is appropriate for the fiber quality you're getting. Price benchmarks are anchored to value retailers (Uniqlo, Target), not to what brands typically charge for that fiber. A $148 acrylic sweater is compared against what that composition actually costs to produce at fair margin — not against the average market price for sweaters.

Material Durability Score

Fiber properties

Each fiber is assigned four property scores (0–100, higher = better performance) sourced from ASTM test standards and Lenzing AG technical data sheets:

  • Pilling resistance — ASTM D4970/D4970M-22. How readily the fiber surface forms pills under abrasion.
  • Tensile strength — ASTM D5034. Force required to break a fabric sample; proxy for tear and wear resistance.
  • Colorfastness — ASTM D4838. Resistance to fading from washing, light, and rubbing.
  • Moisture management — Absorbency and wicking relative to the garment category.

Blend scoring

Blended garments are not scored as a simple weighted average — certain combinations produce interaction effects that differ from the component fibers alone. For example, polyester blended with cotton produces better pilling resistance than either fiber's weighted average because the polyester fibers reinforce the cotton surface. Known interactions are applied as adjustments to the weighted base score.

Category weights

Different garment types place different demands on fibers. Pilling matters most in sweaters (worn against skin and other surfaces repeatedly). Tensile strength matters most in jeans (stress at seams, stretch zones). Moisture management carries the most weight in t-shirts and activewear, where breathability is core to the garment. Fabric weight (GSM)

Fabric weight, measured in grams per square metre (GSM), is captured when a product page lists it. _extract_gsm() scans the product's detail blocks for a stated GSM value (or an ounces-per-square-yard figure it converts). When found, a weight modifier of up to ±10 points is applied to the material score for cotton- or linen-dominant t-shirts and dresses, where weight strongly predicts durability:

  • Under 140 GSM — −10 (lightweight; likely to thin and wear quickly)
  • 140–179 GSM — −5
  • 180–239 GSM — 0 (typical mid-weight baseline)
  • 240–299 GSM — +6
  • 300 GSM and above — +10 (heavyweight; associated with longer wear)

When GSM is not listed for a cotton/linen-dominant t-shirt or dress, the base score may be optimistic for a lightweight version, so a small −3 adjustment is applied to reflect that uncertainty. This is separate from the weight modifier above.

Price Pressure

Price pressure compares the asking price against a benchmark range for the garment's material durability score and category. Benchmarks are anchored to value retailers — what a garment with this fiber profile actually costs from a quality-focused value brand like Uniqlo or Target. This is not what the brand charges; it is what the composition justifies.

Price pressure levels:

  • Undercut — price is below 50% of the benchmark floor. The verdict becomes "price suggests quality tradeoffs": prices this low usually mean construction shortcuts the fiber label won't show, so a low price isn't read as a straightforward deal
  • Low — price is within or below the benchmark range
  • Moderate — up to 50% above benchmark
  • High — 50–150% above benchmark
  • Extreme — more than 150% above benchmark

Worth-It Score

Worth-It Score = Material Score − Price Pressure Penalty + Construction Contribution
Penalty: Low = 0 pts  ·  Moderate = 5 pts  ·  High = 15 pts  ·  Extreme = 25 pts
Construction: (construction score − 5) × 0.8, capped at ±5 pts

A high material score with low price pressure produces a high Worth-It Score. The same fiber composition at triple the fair price produces a low one. The construction estimate then nudges the result up or down — see the Construction Score section below. Score bands:

  • 66–100 — Good to excellent value
  • 41–65 — Average — price premium may or may not be justified
  • 0–40 — You are paying a significant premium for the fiber quality you're getting

Construction Score

Construction quality is scored on a 0–10 scale and folded into the Worth-It Score using the formula above: (construction score − 5) × 0.8, capped at ±5 points. A score of 5 is neutral; higher pushes the Worth-It Score up, lower pulls it down. When the construction estimate is low-confidence, the contribution is halved.

The estimate is built from up to three signals, in increasing order of confidence:

  • Price floor (low confidence) — at a given price and category, certain constructions are economically unlikely. This sets a baseline expectation when no other signal is available.
  • Description text (medium confidence) — construction terms in the product copy (French seams, full lining, double-stitched, serged, plastic hardware) adjust the score, capped at ±3.
  • Product image (confidence set by the image) — when an image is analysed, visible seam, hem, lining, and hardware details adjust the score.

A brand prior may also nudge the construction estimate by a small, capped amount (no more than ±1.5 points) based on a brand's typical construction tier. Brand grades are used only as a minor input and are not published here.

Technical Gear Override

For performance items — waterproof shells, insulated outerwear, and similar technical gear — value is driven by membrane technology, durable water-repellent (DWR) finishes, and seam sealing far more than by fiber composition. Scoring those items on fiber durability alone would be misleading.

When two or more categories of technical signal are detected (for example a membrane brand plus seam sealing), the card applies a technical override: it suppresses the normal price-versus-fiber value judgment and notes that the item's value rests on its construction and technology, which this tool does not attempt to grade.

Confidence Levels

Not all inputs produce equally reliable scores. Confidence is assessed from three signals:

  • High — full composition listed with percentages, all fibers recognized
  • Medium — known fibers but blend interactions are uncharacterized, or GSM is unknown for weight-sensitive fibers like cotton
  • Low — unknown fibers in the composition, or no composition data provided

Confidence affects how much weight you should place on the score. A Low confidence score reflects our best estimate from partial data — treat it accordingly.

When there isn't enough to judge. If the price can't be confirmed or the fiber data is too incomplete to score, the card says "not enough information" rather than forcing a verdict. Honest uncertainty is more useful than a confident wrong answer — we'd rather tell you we can't call it than guess.

Known Limitations

  • GSM is only available when listed. Fabric weight strongly affects durability — a 180 GSM cotton t-shirt outlasts a 130 GSM one with the same composition. We capture and apply GSM when a product page states it, but many pages don't, and in those cases the score carries extra uncertainty (see Fabric Weight above).
  • Construction is estimated, not inspected. Construction quality is scored from price tier and any signals in the description or image, then folded into the Worth-It Score. Without physical inspection it remains an estimate — two garments with identical fiber composition but different seam types, stitch density, and lining can still differ in real-world durability.
  • Fiber scores represent class averages. A high-grade merino will outperform a low-grade merino despite both being scored as "merino."
  • Price benchmarks are calibrated to the US market. Benchmarks may not reflect pricing in other markets.
  • Fiber extraction is AI-assisted. When you scan a URL or paste product text, GPT-4o-mini extracts the fiber composition. Extraction errors are possible, especially with unusual formatting. Always verify against the source if precision matters.
