worth it? methodology

Every score on this site is derived from published fiber science, not opinion. This page documents exactly how scores are calculated so you can scrutinize the methodology, identify where confidence is limited, and hold us accountable. v1.0

What scores measure — and what they don't

What fiber science can tell you (high confidence): how a specific fiber composition behaves over time — pilling rate, tensile strength, colorfastness, moisture management. These properties are measurable by ASTM standards and are consistent across garments made from the same fiber.

What fiber science cannot tell you: how well a specific garment was constructed. A $20 cotton t-shirt and a $120 cotton t-shirt share the same fiber score. Construction quality — seam type, stitch density, lining — requires physical inspection and is not captured in the Material Durability score.

What price pressure tells you: whether the price you're paying is appropriate for the fiber quality you're getting. Price benchmarks are anchored to value retailers (Uniqlo, Target), not to what brands typically charge for that fiber. A $148 acrylic sweater is compared against what that composition actually costs to produce at fair margin — not against the average market price for sweaters.

Material Durability Score

Fiber properties

Each fiber is assigned four property scores (0–100, higher = better performance) sourced from ASTM test standards and Lenzing AG technical data sheets:

  • Pilling resistance — ASTM D4970/D4970M-22. How readily the fiber surface forms pills under abrasion.
  • Tensile strength — ASTM D5034. Force required to break a fabric sample; proxy for tear and wear resistance.
  • Colorfastness — ASTM D4838. Resistance to fading from washing, light, and rubbing.
  • Moisture management — Absorbency and wicking relative to the garment category.

Blend scoring

Blended garments are not scored as a simple weighted average — certain combinations produce interaction effects that differ from the component fibers alone. For example, polyester blended with cotton produces better pilling resistance than either fiber's weighted average because the polyester fibers reinforce the cotton surface. Known interactions are applied as adjustments to the weighted base score.

Price Pressure

Price pressure compares the asking price against a benchmark range for the garment's material durability score and category. Benchmarks are anchored to value retailers — what a garment with this fiber profile actually costs from a quality-focused value brand like Uniqlo or Target. This is not what the brand charges; it is what the composition justifies.

Price pressure levels:

  • Low — price is within or below the benchmark range
  • Moderate — up to 50% above benchmark
  • High — 50–150% above benchmark
  • Extreme — more than 150% above benchmark

Worth-It Score

Worth-It Score = Material Score − Price Pressure Penalty
Penalty: Low = 0 pts  ·  Moderate = 5 pts  ·  High = 15 pts  ·  Extreme = 25 pts

A high material score with low price pressure produces a high Worth-It Score. The same fiber composition at triple the fair price produces a low one. Score bands:

  • 66–100 — Good to excellent value
  • 41–65 — Average — price premium may or may not be justified
  • 0–40 — You are paying a significant premium for the fiber quality you're getting

Confidence Levels

Not all inputs produce equally reliable scores. Confidence is assessed from three signals:

  • High — full composition listed with percentages, all fibers recognized
  • Medium — known fibers but blend interactions are uncharacterized, or GSM is unknown for weight-sensitive fibers like cotton
  • Low — unknown fibers in the composition, or no composition data provided

Confidence affects how much weight you should place on the score. A Low confidence score reflects our best estimate from partial data — treat it accordingly.

Known Limitations

  • GSM is not captured. A 180 GSM cotton t-shirt is more durable than a 130 GSM cotton t-shirt with the same composition. We do not currently have GSM data and note this in medium-confidence scores.
  • Construction quality is not scored. Two garments with identical fiber composition but different seam types, stitch density, and lining will have different real-world durability. This is not yet captured.
  • Fiber scores represent class averages. A high-grade merino will outperform a low-grade merino despite both being scored as "merino."
  • Price benchmarks are calibrated to the US market. Benchmarks may not reflect pricing in other markets.
  • Fiber extraction is AI-assisted. When you scan a URL or paste product text, GPT-4o-mini extracts the fiber composition. Extraction errors are possible, especially with unusual formatting. Always verify against the source if precision matters.
